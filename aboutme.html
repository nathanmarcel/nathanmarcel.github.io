About Me

I’m originally from the SF Bay Area, and I completed my BSME & MSME from Purdue University (Spring 2022/Spring 2023 respectively). Currently, I work at Medtronic in Boston as a part of their surgical robotics division.

The robot I work on at Medtronic is called the Hugo RAS System, which is a minimally invasive soft-tissue robot. I’ve previously worked on Johnson & Johnson’s bronchoscopy & urology robot Monarch, as well as Stryker’s orthopedic robot MAKO in past internships. (Not) to flex, but I may be one of the only people in the world who have worked on 3 surgical robots in their career (and are under the age of 30).

I am also extremely passionate about comedy. I performed stand-up in college (and am planning on returning in the near future), and I also try to write comedic poetry now and then (which I would post on this website if I thought it was any good, or funny). I’ve found that these hobbies are a bit unusual for someone in tech. Not to hate on other engineers, but we tend to be relatively serious, fact-driven people. I, however, am extremely unserious and sarcastic, which leads to situations at work where my colleagues mistake me for an insane person when I’m trying to do a bit about absolute vs relative encoders at lunch (though they may be right). A goal of mine is to combine my two passions for robotics & comedy somehow. I’ve been working on a series of educational videos called “robotics without the bullshit” to teach the fundamentals of the field without any of the bullshit I had to endure via a traditional education, such as violently long math/mathematical proofs, technical jargon, or homework.

Another dream of mine is to someday launch a ‘Robotics Institute’, or a 1-2 year school where students literally just work in teams and build robots. I think that while there is value in pursuing a degree in some engineering discipline (and I definitely don’t regret it), for the most part, you aren’t taught how the industry works or how to build anything practical. (quick rant ->) In reality, you show up to a lecture hall where a professor derives an equation for an hour, then you go home to do the homework where you end up using 6 other equations (which are different than the one the professor covered), and then after a few months you take an exam where you use another 3 different ones. It’s true that universities do exist more for research than teaching students, but am I really crazy to ask that they teach us more practical things when we pay them tens of thousands of dollars every year to fund their experiments? No?

I should probably wrap this up, I’m not good enough at HTML to know how to make this page full of text look nice. I’m also a big basketball fan (yes I’m a Warriors fan, and yes I know they’re not as good as they used to be but the only reason you have the courage to point that out is because you’re not scared of them anymore). I 3D print random things around my place, I really like cooking (Mexican + Indian fusion is my jam), and I know way too much about the extended lore of the Halo series.
